Voice morphs

Voice morphs are sounds whose acoustic features lie in-between those of two real-world (or veridical) sounds, one of which is vocal (e.g., clear or whispered speech, laughter, crying) and the other is non-vocal (e.g., music, ringtones, boiling water).

Each row in the table below contains example sounds from a different voice-morph continuum. Sounds in the first and last columns are the vocal and non-vocal veridical recordings, respectively, which were used to create the morphs. The second and second-to-last columns are synthesized versions of the original sounds, which were designed to sound as similar to the original recordings as possible. The remaining columns contain 0%, 20%, 50%, 80%, 100% morphs.

In some cases, the global frequencies of the sinusoidal components of the synthesized sounds were shifted up or down prior to morphing. This was done so that the two ends of the continuum had roughly the same pitch, greatly improving the pleasantness of the subsequent morphs. The consequence of this frequency shifting can be heard between columns 2 and 3, and between columns 7 and 8.
